Fibrinogen (I) → Fibrin (Ia)

Thrombin cleaves fibrinopeptides A and B from the fibrinogen molecule, creating fibrin monomers. The monomers spontaneously polymerize end-to-end and side-to-side, forming a SOFT (soluble) fibrin clot held together by hydrogen bonds.

Fibrinogen is the MAIN SUBSTRATE of the coagulation cascade — reference range: 200-400 mg/dL
💡 Thrombin splits fibrinopeptides A and B from the alpha and beta chains of fibrinogen. The resulting fibrin monomers polymerize spontaneously. At this stage the clot is still 'soluble' — it can be dissolved by 5M urea or 1% monochloroacetic acid. This is used in the Factor XIII screening test. Fibrinogen is an acute-phase reactant — elevated in inflammation, infection, pregnancy.
Fibrin Formation Steps (Fig. 15.7):
1. Thrombin cleaves fibrinopeptides A & B from fibrinogen
2. Fibrin monomers polymerize (hydrogen bonds) → soft/soluble clot
3. Factor XIIIa cross-links with covalent bonds → stable/insoluble clot
4. Cross-linked clot resists fibrinolysis (plasmin)

Clinical & BOC Exam Notes

Common Pathway & Testing
Common pathway factors (X, V, II, I) are evaluated by BOTH PT and aPTT. If both tests are prolonged → suspect common pathway deficiency, Vitamin K deficiency, warfarin, liver disease, or DIC. If only PT is prolonged → extrinsic (Factor VII). If only aPTT → intrinsic (XII, XI, IX, VIII).
DIC — Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation
DIC causes simultaneous clotting AND bleeding. Lab findings: prolonged PT, prolonged aPTT, decreased fibrinogen, decreased platelets, elevated D-dimer, elevated FDPs, decreased Factor V & VIII. Causes: sepsis, obstetric complications (abruptio placentae), acute promyelocytic leukemia (APL/M3), trauma, malignancy.
D-Dimer vs. FDPs
D-dimer is SPECIFIC for cross-linked fibrin degradation (requires XIIIa action first). FDPs detect degradation of both fibrinogen AND fibrin. In DIC, BOTH are elevated. D-dimer is used to rule out DVT/PE — a negative D-dimer has high negative predictive value.
Direct Oral Anticoagulants (DOACs)
Apixaban & Rivaroxaban → direct Factor Xa inhibitors. Dabigatran → direct thrombin (IIa) inhibitor. DOACs do NOT require routine monitoring (unlike warfarin/heparin). BOC loves asking which factor each DOAC targets.
Fibrinogen Testing — Clauss Method
Clauss fibrinogen: add excess thrombin to diluted plasma → measure time to clot → inversely proportional to fibrinogen concentration. Reference range: 200-400 mg/dL. Afibrinogenemia: 0 mg/dL. Hypofibrinogenemia (heterozygous): 20-100 mg/dL. Dysfibrinogenemia: functional vs. antigenic level disparity.
Factor V Leiden & APC Resistance
Factor V Leiden mutation → mutant Factor V cannot be inactivated by Activated Protein C (APC). This is the most common inherited thrombophilia. Factor V activity level is NORMAL — the mutation affects APC's cleavage site, not Factor V production. Leads to increased thrombotic risk.
Thrombin Time (TT)
The thrombin time adds thrombin directly to patient plasma and measures fibrinogen → fibrin conversion. Prolonged by: low fibrinogen, dysfibrinogenemia, heparin, FDPs (they interfere with fibrin polymerization), and direct thrombin inhibitors. TT bypasses the intrinsic and extrinsic pathways entirely.
