Clinical & BOC Exam Notes

aPTT Monitoring
The aPTT evaluates the intrinsic + common pathways. Patient plasma + phospholipid + contact activator + Ca²⁺ → clot. Normal aPTT: ~25-35 seconds. Prolonged aPTT with normal PT → intrinsic pathway defect (VIII, IX, XI, or XII).
Hemophilia A vs. B — High-Yield BOC
Hemophilia A (Factor VIII deficiency) = most common severe bleeding disorder (~80% of hemophilia). Hemophilia B (Factor IX deficiency) = Christmas Disease (~15%). Both are X-linked recessive, both show prolonged aPTT with normal PT. Differentiation requires specific factor assays.
Contact Factor Deficiency — Classic Trick Question
Factor XII (Hageman) deficiency causes a markedly prolonged aPTT but NO clinical bleeding. This is the most tested concept — students expect prolonged aPTT = bleeding, but contact factors are not required for in vivo hemostasis. Factor XII deficiency is actually associated with THROMBOSIS.
Factor VIII & von Willebrand Factor
Factor VIII circulates bound to vWF — vWF is its carrier protein. In von Willebrand disease, Factor VIII levels may be decreased because without vWF, Factor VIII is rapidly degraded. This is why vWD can cause a prolonged aPTT.
Heparin & the aPTT
Unfractionated heparin (UFH) potentiates antithrombin → inhibits thrombin (IIa) and Factor Xa. Monitored by aPTT. LMWH primarily inhibits Xa and is monitored by anti-Xa assay, NOT aPTT. BOC loves this distinction.
Mixing Studies
Prolonged aPTT → perform 1:1 mixing study with normal plasma. If aPTT CORRECTS → factor deficiency. If aPTT does NOT correct → inhibitor present (lupus anticoagulant or specific factor inhibitor). Classic BOC format.
